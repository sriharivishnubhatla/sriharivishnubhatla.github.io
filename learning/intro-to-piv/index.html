An Introduction to Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV)

5 Apr, 2026
|

Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is one of the most powerful, non-intrusive optical measurement techniques used in experimental fluid mechanics today. Instead of inserting a physical probe (like a pitot tube) that disturbs the flow, PIV allows us to capture the instantaneous velocity fields of an entire 2D plane or 3D volume simultaneously.

At its core, PIV is based on the simplest equation in kinematics:

V=ΔxΔt\mathbf{V} = \frac{\Delta \mathbf{x}}{\Delta t}V=ΔtΔx​

If we know how far a particle has traveled (Δx\Delta \mathbf{x}Δx) over a known time interval (Δt\Delta tΔt), we know its velocity (V\mathbf{V}V). PIV is simply the highly sophisticated, high-speed engineering required to capture those two variables accurately.

The Core Components of a PIV Setup

To capture these measurements, a standard 2D-PIV experiment requires four main physical components working in perfect unison.

Seeding Particles

Fluids like air and water are transparent. To see the flow, we must seed it with tracer particles. These particles must be small enough to faithfully follow the flow dynamics without lagging, but large enough to scatter sufficient light to the camera. Common materials include olive oil droplets for air flows, or silver-coated hollow glass spheres for water.

The Illumination Source (Laser)

We illuminate a specific slice of the flow using a high-powered laser (typically an Nd:YAG laser). The laser beam is passed through cylindrical optics to form a thin “laser sheet.” This ensures we are only illuminating particles within a tightly defined 2D plane.

The Imaging Device

A high-resolution, high-speed camera sits perpendicular to the laser sheet. When the laser fires, the camera captures the light scattered by the seeding particles. For standard PIV, the camera must capture two images in rapid succession (a “frame pair”).

The Synchronizer (Programmed Trigger Unit)

This is the brain of the operation. The laser pulse and the camera shutter must be synchronized down to the microsecond. A Programmed Trigger Unit (PTU) sends precise TTL pulses to fire the laser and open the camera shutter at the exact right moment.

Note on Custom Hardware: While commercial synchronizers are expensive, it is entirely possible to design a custom PTU using microcontrollers like Arduino to handle precise sub-millisecond triggering. I will cover custom PTU design in a future lab note.

Image Processing and Cross-Correlation

Once we have our image pair, separated by the known time interval Δt\Delta tΔt, the hardware’s job is done, and the algorithmic work begins.

We do not track individual particles. Instead, the images are divided into small, overlapping grid squares called Interrogation Windows (e.g., 32x32 pixels).

The software applies a mathematical operation called Cross-Correlation to compare a window in Image A to the corresponding window in Image B.

The discrete cross-correlation function computes the spatial shift that maximizes the match between the particle patterns in both windows:

R(x,y)=∑i=0N−1∑j=0M−1IA(i,j)IB(i+x,j+y)R(x,y) = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \sum_{j=0}^{M-1} I_A(i,j) I_B(i+x, j+y)R(x,y)=∑i=0N−1​∑j=0M−1​IA​(i,j)IB​(i+x,j+y)

The peak of this correlation map gives us the most probable displacement vector (Δx\Delta \mathbf{x}Δx) for that specific group of particles.

Post-Processing and Validation

After calculating the displacement vectors for every interrogation window, we obtain a raw vector field. However, optical noise, reflections off test facility walls, or poor seeding density can lead to spurious vectors.

The final step involves:

  • Validation: Removing outliers using techniques like the normalized median test.
  • Segmentation & Refinement: Advanced techniques exist to segment particles from noisy backgrounds before correlation even happens, drastically improving the signal-to-noise ratio in challenging environments.

What’s Next?

This basic 2D-PIV configuration forms the foundation for more advanced techniques, such as Stereo-PIV (capturing all three velocity components in a plane) and Tomographic PIV (capturing the full 3D volume).
