PLIF

Planar Laser-Induced Fluorescence (PLIF) is a powerful, non-intrusive optical diagnostic technique used to map the concentration of a scalar—such as a dye, species, or temperature—in a fluid flow. While Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) reveals the kinematics of the flow, PLIF allows us to understand transport phenomena, turbulent mixing, and dispersion of species.

PLIF works on a very simple physical principle:

  • Excitation: A light source, typically a laser sheet, illuminates molecules of a fluorescent dye within the flow field.
  • Absorption: The dye molecules absorb the incoming photons and jump to higher, unstable energy states.
  • Fluorescence: The excited molecules rapidly decay back to their ground state, emitting light at longer wavelengths.
  • Detection: This emitted fluorescence is captured perpendicular to the laser sheet by a high-speed camera typically equipped with spectral long pass filters.

The process is essentially “lights, action & camera”!

The intensity of the captured fluorescence is directly related to the local concentration of the dye, allowing us to create a 2D (sometimes even 3D) map of scalar concentration across the flow field.

The dye is selected such that the emission peak is usually well separated from the excitation wavelength to ensure that only fluorescence is captured by the camera (this is where the use of a cut off filter comes in). For example, Rhodamine 6G (R6G) is a commonly used dye in PLIF experiments, with an absorption peak around 530 nm (perfect for excitation by green lasers at 532nm) and an emission peak around 550-600 nm. This spectral separation allows for effective filtering of the excitation light, ensuring that the camera captures only the fluorescence signal.

The Governing Equation

The fluorescence intensity (FFF) captured by our camera pixels depends on the following critical factors:

  1. Local concentration of the fluorescing species (CCC)
  2. Temperature and pressure of the fluid
  3. Local laser intensity (III)
  4. Quantum yield of the molecule (the efficiency of the fluorescence process)

Assuming temperature and pressure are constant in a standard isothermal liquid flow, the relationship simplifies to a linear equation:

F(x,y)∝I(x,y)⋅C(x,y)\large F(x,y) \propto I(x,y) \cdot C(x,y)F(x,y)∝I(x,y)⋅C(x,y)

or

F(x,y)=k⋅I(x,y)⋅C(x,y)\large F(x,y) = k \cdot I(x,y) \cdot C(x,y)F(x,y)=k⋅I(x,y)⋅C(x,y)

Here, kkk is a calibration constant that lumps together the quantum yield, the absorption coefficient of the dye, and the optical efficiency of the camera setup and any other parameter that is random but is not expected to change in a given experiment. It is essential to perform the experiments in the linear region of FFF vs CCC. At higher concentrations, there will be a saturation region where the linear relationship no longer holds and PLIF cannot be accurately performed.

A sample calibration plot illustrating this critical behavior is shown below:

This figure demonstrates why maintaining a low dye concentration is vital. If the concentration exceeds the linear limit, increases in local scalar concentration will not result in corresponding increases in detected light, leading to significant quantitative errors.

However, the tradeoff with lower concentrations is the lower dynamic range. Therefore, an optimal working concentration range must be chosen to balance the need for a strong signal with the requirement for linearity.

To find the concentration map of the flow, we rearrange the equation, assuming we are operating within the linear region:

C(x,y)=F(x,y)k⋅I(x,y)\large C(x,y) = \frac{F(x,y)}{k \cdot I(x,y)}C(x,y)=k⋅I(x,y)F(x,y)​

The Attenuation Problem

Looking at the equation above, calculating concentration seems trivial: divide the captured image (FFF) by the laser intensity (III) and the constant (kkk).

However, there is a fundamental complication: III is not constant and we do not know the variation of I(x,y)I(x,y)I(x,y) in space.

As the laser sheet propagates through the measurement section, the fluorescent dye absorbs the laser energy. Consequently, the laser sheet gets progressively dimmer the deeper it penetrates into the fluid. This is known as Laser Attenuation.

To understand this physically, consider the loss of illumination intensity in an infinitesimally small control volume (a 1D element along the laser path). The change of intensity (ΔI\Delta IΔI) across this element is proportional to the width of the element (Δx\Delta xΔx), the local illumination intensity (III), and the local concentration (CCC):

ΔI∝−I⋅C⋅Δx\Delta I \propto - I \cdot C \cdot \Delta xΔI∝−I⋅C⋅Δx

By introducing the proportionality constant ζ\zetaζ (zeta), which represents the attenuation coefficient, we can write the differential form:

dI=−ζ⋅I⋅C⋅dxdI = -\zeta \cdot I \cdot C \cdot dxdI=−ζ⋅I⋅C⋅dx

Integrating this relationship from the entry point (x=0x=0x=0, where intensity is I0I_0I0​) to a depth xxx, we arrive at the governing Beer-Lambert law for a varying concentration field:

I(x)=I0exp⁡(−∫0xζ⋅C(x′) dx′)I(x) = I_0 \exp\left( -\int_0^x \zeta \cdot C(x') \, dx' \right)I(x)=I0​exp(−∫0x​ζ⋅C(x′)dx′)

If we naively assume the laser intensity is constant across the entire field of view, our calculated concentration CCC will be artificially low in regions of high attenuation. As detailed in our recent work on the subject, ignoring or improperly correcting for this optical phenomenon leads to severe spatial errors in concentration mapping [1].

Note that if a low value of concentration is used, the resulting measurements will lack dynamic range, if higher values are used, the attenuation becomes predominant. Therefore, we need to understand that it is all a huge optimization problem.

Handling Divergent Sheets via Projective Transform

Since the camera is a cartesian grid, we would be integrating to correct for attenuation in cartesian coordinates. In reality, most laser sheets are radially divergent, not perfectly parallel. To apply the explicit marching process to a divergent sheet, we must either use a combination of divergent and convergent lenses as shown below to get a parallel sheet.

In the absence of such facility, we must apply a Projective Transform. This mathematical operation stretches the raw image to remove the divergence, effectively converting the diverging sheet into a parallel Cartesian array. The distance and the geometric drop in intensity due to beam divergence must be carefully compensated for during this transformation. Once the image is projected into a parallel sheet, the marching process can begin from the reference cell. See ref [3] for more details on projective transform.

Techniques for Attenuation Compensation

To accurately map concentration, we must correct for the attenuation. Depending on the experimental setup, there are distinct mathematical approaches to solve this coupled inverse problem.

The Explicit Marching Process (With a Reference Cell)

The most accurate method for estimating the concentration field requires a reference cell (a region where both the fluorescence intensity and the exact concentration are known) and a parallel light sheet.

By knowing the starting laser intensity at the reference cell, we can calculate the exact concentration and remaining laser energy pixel-by-pixel. To understand this mathematically, imagine the parallel laser sheet traveling along the yyy-axis. We discretize this path into individual pixels of width Δy\Delta yΔy, indexed by i=0,1,2,...,Ni = 0, 1, 2, ..., Ni=0,1,2,...,N.

1. The Reference Cell (Pixel 0): The laser enters the field at the reference cell (Pixel 0) with a known, unattenuated intensity I0I_0I0​. Because the camera captures the fluorescence F0F_0F0​ at this pixel, we can calculate the exact concentration C0C_0C0​: C0=F0k⋅I0C_0 = \frac{F_0}{k \cdot I_0}C0​=k⋅I0​F0​​

2. Marching to Pixel 1: As the laser passes through Pixel 0, it is absorbed by the dye concentration C0C_0C0​. According to the discretized Beer-Lambert law, the intensity of the laser drops exponentially over the width of the pixel (Δy\Delta yΔy). The laser entering Pixel 1 has an intensity of: I1=I0exp⁡(−ϵ⋅C0⋅Δy)I_1 = I_0 \exp(-\epsilon \cdot C_0 \cdot \Delta y)I1​=I0​exp(−ϵ⋅C0​⋅Δy) (Where ϵ\epsilonϵ is the absorption coefficient of the dye, equivalent to ζ\zetaζ above).

Now that we know I1I_1I1​ and the camera provides the measured fluorescence F1F_1F1​, we can calculate the concentration at Pixel 1: C1=F1k⋅I1C_1 = \frac{F_1}{k \cdot I_1}C1​=k⋅I1​F1​​

3. The General Recurrence Relation: By continuing this process—marching along the row pixel by pixel—we can calculate the state of the entire flow field. For any given pixel nnn, the local concentration relies on the laser intensity InI_nIn​, which is calculated from the concentration of the immediately preceding pixel:

In=In−1exp⁡(−ϵ⋅Cn−1⋅Δy)I_n = I_{n-1} \exp(-\epsilon \cdot C_{n-1} \cdot \Delta y)In​=In−1​exp(−ϵ⋅Cn−1​⋅Δy) Cn=Fnk⋅InC_n = \frac{F_n}{k \cdot I_n}Cn​=k⋅In​Fn​​

4. The Cumulative Intensity Map: If we unroll this recurrence relation, we can express the laser intensity at any pixel nnn as a function of the incoming laser I0I_0I0​ and the cumulative sum of all the concentration the beam has passed through up to that point:

In=I0exp⁡(−ϵ⋅Δy∑i=0n−1Ci)I_n = I_0 \exp\left( -\epsilon \cdot \Delta y \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} C_i \right)In​=I0​exp(−ϵ⋅Δy∑i=0n−1​Ci​)

This elegant marching process yields highly accurate results because it directly computes the physical loss of light at every discrete step. However, its strict reliance on an optical reference cell and perfectly parallel light sheets makes it challenging to deploy in standard experimental test rigs.

The Iterative Technique (Absence of a Reference Cell)

In most practical experimental setups, inserting a reference cell into the field of view is impossible or highly disruptive. In the absence of a reference cell, we implement an Iterative Technique to mathematically estimate the concentration field using the Beer-Lambert law.

According to the Beer-Lambert law, the attenuated laser intensity I(y)I(y)I(y) at any depth yyy along the laser path is a function of the integral of the concentration it has passed through:

I(y)=I0exp⁡(−ϵ∫0yC(y′)dy′)I(y) = I_0 \exp\left(-\epsilon \int_0^y C(y') dy'\right)I(y)=I0​exp(−ϵ∫0y​C(y′)dy′)

Where I0I_0I0​ is the unattenuated, incoming laser intensity and ϵ\epsilonϵ is the absorption coefficient of the dye. Because I(y)I(y)I(y) and C(y)C(y)C(y) depend on one another, we solve for them iteratively:

1. Initial Guess (Iteration 0): We assume zero attenuation. The preliminary concentration field C0(y)C_0(y)C0​(y) is calculated using the unattenuated laser profile I0I_0I0​: C0(y)=F(y)k⋅I0C_0(y) = \frac{F(y)}{k \cdot I_0}C0​(y)=k⋅I0​F(y)​

2. Compute Attenuated Field: For the nnn-th iteration, we calculate how much the laser would attenuate as it travels through the concentration field estimated in the previous step (Cn−1C_{n-1}Cn−1​): In(y)=I0exp⁡(−ϵ∫0yCn−1(y′)dy′)I_n(y) = I_0 \exp\left(-\epsilon \int_0^y C_{n-1}(y') dy'\right)In​(y)=I0​exp(−ϵ∫0y​Cn−1​(y′)dy′)

3. Update Concentration: We calculate the new concentration Cn(y)C_n(y)Cn​(y) by dividing the raw fluorescence image by the updated, attenuated laser map In(y)I_n(y)In​(y): Cn(y)=F(y)k⋅In(y)C_n(y) = \frac{F(y)}{k \cdot I_n(y)}Cn​(y)=k⋅In​(y)F(y)​

4. The Recurrence Relation: By substituting In(y)I_n(y)In​(y) into the concentration equation, the entire iterative loop can be expressed as a single governing recurrence relation: Cn(y)=C0(y)exp⁡(ϵ∫0yCn−1(y′)dy′)C_n(y) = C_0(y) \exp\left(\epsilon \int_0^y C_{n-1}(y') dy'\right)Cn​(y)=C0​(y)exp(ϵ∫0y​Cn−1​(y′)dy′)

This process (Cn→In+1→Cn+1C_n \rightarrow I_{n+1} \rightarrow C_{n+1}Cn​→In+1​→Cn+1​) is repeated until the difference between successive concentration maps falls below a strict error tolerance threshold.

Crucial Limitation: As analyzed in our recent methodological review [2], this iterative loop successfully converges to an accurate concentration field, but the process has a monotonic convergence only when operated on the mean field. Applying it directly to highly fluctuating instantaneous fields without care can lead to convergence issues.

Extension to Gaseous Flows: Quantitative Smoke Visualization

While PLIF relies on fluorescent dye in liquids, the exact same mathematical attenuation-correction methodologies can be applied to gaseous flows seeded with smoke or fog particles. When laser light is scattered by smoke (instead of fluorescing), the signal suffers from the exact same attenuation along the beam path.

By applying this explicit marching or iterative technique to scattered light images in air, we achieve highly accurate concentration measurements of the gas phase, a technique formally known as Quantitative Smoke Visualization (QSV) [3].
